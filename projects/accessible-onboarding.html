Accessibility UX Design WCAG 2.1

Accessible Onboarding

Redesigning a product's onboarding flow to meet WCAG AA — eliminating barriers for users with assistive technology and improving overall task completion by 23%.

Role
UX Designer · Accessibility Lead
Year
2023
Team
2 designers · 3 engineers · 1 QA
Tools
Figma · VoiceOver · NVDA · axe DevTools
Problem Accessibility Audit Design Decisions Testing Outcomes

01 — Problem

A 40% drop-off nobody had investigated.

The existing onboarding flow had a persistent 40% drop-off on the third step. Analytics showed the problem clearly; they didn't show why. When we reviewed support tickets, a pattern emerged — a significant proportion came from users with assistive technology who couldn't complete the flow at all.

The form had no visible focus states. Error messages weren't linked to their inputs. The multi-step progress indicator was decoration, not information. Keyboard users could tab into a modal but couldn't escape it without a mouse.

"I've tried three times. The form submits but nothing happens and there's no feedback from my screen reader." — User feedback ticket

02 — Accessibility Audit

Eighteen failures. None of them surprising.

We ran a structured audit using axe DevTools, manual keyboard testing, and screen reader walkthroughs with VoiceOver (macOS/iOS) and NVDA (Windows). The failures clustered into four clear categories.

A

Focus management

No visible focus indicators anywhere in the flow. Focus didn't move to the modal on open, and the Escape key didn't close it. Tab order didn't follow visual reading order.

B

Form labelling

Several inputs had placeholder text as the only label — which disappears on interaction. Error messages were positioned visually near inputs but not programmatically associated via aria-describedby.

C

Colour contrast

The secondary button text failed 4.5:1 contrast ratio against its background. Helper text at 12px failed large-text thresholds. Status indicators used colour alone to convey meaning.

D

Status & feedback

The progress indicator had no programmatic role or label. Success and error states changed visually but were never announced to screen readers via live regions.

03 — Design Decisions

Fix the foundation. Improve the whole.

Accessibility fixes rarely stay isolated — when you fix a focus state, you're also improving clarity for all users. When you add a visible error message linked to its input, you reduce confusion for everyone. The redesign addressed every audit failure, but also used those fixes as an opportunity to improve the overall UX clarity of the flow.

The progress indicator became a proper progressbar ARIA role with a meaningful label. Every form field got a persistent, visible label above the input. Error messages were rewritten to be specific and actionable, not generic.

Redesigned form with labelling and focus states

A focus ring is not an eyesore — it's the cursor for keyboard users. We designed focus indicators that were visible and on-brand: 2px solid accent green, 3px offset. They made the product look more considered, not less.

04 — Testing

Test with real users. Not just automated tools.

Automated tools catch roughly 30–40% of accessibility issues. The rest require manual testing and, ideally, testing with people who rely on assistive technology daily. We recruited five participants who used screen readers as their primary means of browsing, and conducted moderated usability sessions with the prototype.

Three sessions surfaced issues the automated audit had missed entirely — a custom dropdown that announced incorrectly, a date picker that trapped keyboard focus, and a success animation that caused a WCAG 2.3.3 motion violation.

05 — Outcomes

Better access. Better completion. Better product.

Post-launch metrics showed a 23% improvement in onboarding completion rate overall — not just for users with assistive technology. Support tickets from users encountering errors in the flow dropped by 61% within the first month. The product passed a third-party WCAG 2.1 AA audit with no critical or serious failures.
