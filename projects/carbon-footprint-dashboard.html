Sustainability Data Visualisation UI Design

Carbon Footprint Dashboard

Turning dense carbon emissions data into clear, actionable insight — a sustainability metrics UI designed for non-technical users at a green-tech startup.

Lead UI Designer

01 — Problem

Data that only data scientists could read.

The startup had built a powerful carbon accounting engine — it could ingest emissions data across Scope 1, 2, and 3, model reduction scenarios, and benchmark against industry standards. But the interface had been designed by engineers for engineers. When they started selling to sustainability managers and finance teams, the product fell flat.

The dashboard showed everything at once: thirty-seven data points on a single screen, six different chart types, and a colour system that conveyed no consistent meaning. Users told researchers they "didn't know where to look first" and "weren't sure what they were supposed to do with the information."

"We know the data is right. The problem is our users open the dashboard and immediately feel overwhelmed. They close it and email us instead." — Product Manager

02 — Users

Two jobs. Very different mental models.

Research surfaced two distinct user types with different goals. Sustainability Managers wanted to understand their current position, identify their biggest opportunities, and communicate progress to the board. They needed clear narrative — a story the data was telling. Finance Directors wanted to verify numbers, track trends over time, and understand liability. They needed specificity and audit trails.

The original dashboard served neither. We designed for both — starting with the sustainability manager as the primary user, with progressive disclosure paths into the detail that finance users needed.

03 — IA & Hierarchy

One number to rule them all.

The most important design decision was reducing the entry point to a single headline metric: total emissions this period, with a clear indicator of whether this is better or worse than last period. Everything else is secondary.

We structured the information architecture around three questions users actually asked: "How are we doing overall?" → "Where are our biggest emissions?" → "What can we do about it?". Each question had a corresponding section, and navigation flowed in that order.

1

Overview strip

Total emissions, period-over-period change, and target tracking. Three numbers. Clear status. Immediately scannable.

2

Breakdown by scope

Scope 1, 2, and 3 visualised comparatively — not as separate charts but as a single proportional view that shows where the weight sits.

3

Top emission sources

Ranked list of the five largest contributing categories — with reduction potential clearly indicated alongside each.

4

Trend & forecast

Historical trend with projected trajectory at current pace — and a "what if" layer for modelling reduction scenarios.

04 — Chart Design

Less chart types. More clarity.

We reduced from six chart types to three: a stacked area chart for trends over time, a horizontal bar chart for comparisons, and a simple progress arc for target tracking. Each was chosen because it matched the cognitive task — not because the library offered it.

Colour was given systematic meaning: green for on-track or improved, amber for at-risk, and a neutral palette for everything else. Red was reserved only for "exceeded limit" states. Colour was never the only signal — every status had a text label and an icon.

Dashboard chart system

Every chart passes WCAG 1.4.11 non-text contrast, and the colour system works in greyscale — tested by printing the dashboard in black and white. If the meaning survives the print, colour is doing its job as enhancement, not as the sole carrier of information.

05 — Sustainable UI

A sustainability product should model what it preaches.

Given the product's purpose, it felt important to apply green design principles to the interface itself. We minimised JavaScript dependencies for chart rendering, choosing a lightweight library over a feature-heavy one. Dark mode reduced OLED screen energy consumption — and given sustainability managers were often viewing dashboards in board meetings on large screens, it also reduced presentation glare.

We used the Website Carbon Calculator to benchmark the dashboard's own emissions before and after — the redesigned version was 68% lighter than the original.

06 — Outcomes

Clear data. Engaged users. Fewer support calls.

Three months after launch, weekly active usage of dashboard features had nearly quadrupled. Users were spending more time exploring the "what can we do about it" section — the reduction opportunities — which was the behaviour the product team had always wanted but never achieved.

The product was featured in two climate-tech publications as an example of data visualisation done with restraint. The CEO cited the redesign as a significant factor in their next funding round conversations.
